Remember that there are some congruence theorems that are short cuts for checking congruence between two triangles:
\begin{itemize}
\item SSS (side-side-side): all three sides of both triangles are congruent
\item SAS (side-angle-side): the two triangles have two sides congruent and the angle between those sides is also congruent
\item ASA (angle-side-angle): the two triangles have two angles congruent and the side between those angles is also congruent
\item AAS (angle-angle-side): the two triangles have two angles congruent and the side not between those angles is also congruent
\item HL (hypotenuse-leg): two right triangles are congruent if their hypotenuses are congruent and one of their legs is also congruent. [This is a special case of SSS!]
\end{itemize}


Let's use these triangle congruence theorems to investigate some properties of quadrilaterals.


\begin{problem}
	A quadrilateral has diagonals that are perpendicular. Does this shape always have to be a rhombus? 
	
	Investigate by first drawing some pictures. If the answer is no, you should draw an example of a shape that is not a rhombus. If the answer is yes, you should use the triangle congruence theorems to prove that the shape must be a rhombus.
 
\end{problem}


\begin{problem}
A quadrilateral has diagonals that are perpendicular bisectors of one another. Is this quadrilateral always a rhombus?


Investigate by first drawing some pictures. If the answer is no, you should draw an example of a shape that is not a rhombus. If the answer is yes, you should use the triangle congruence theorems to prove that the shape must be a rhombus.
\end{problem}




\begin{problem}
A quadrilateral is a rhombus. Is this quadrilateral always a parallelogram?


Investigate by first drawing some pictures. If the answer is no, you should draw an example of a shape that is a rhombus but is not a parallelogram. If the answer is yes, you should use the triangle congruence theorems to prove that the shape must be a parallelogram.
\end{problem}


\begin{problem}
If we have a property that is true for rhombuses, does that property always have to be true for squares? Explain how you know, drawing pictures as needed to illustrate your thinking.
\end{problem}


\begin{problem}
If you get done early, pick another property of quadrilaterals (look back at Property Rights for some examples!) and prove them using triangle congruence.
\end{problem}

\begin{instructorNotes}
{\bf Main goal: } The goal in this activity is to showcase how the congruence theorems can help us prove some of the properties we've observed.


{\bf Overall picture:} When we finish with this activity, we should be able to see that some properties can be inherited with the category we are discussing. For instance, squares inherit all of the properties of rhombuses as in problem 4.  This can be a tough activity, since it deals with some logic which is difficult for many students.


\begin{itemize}
	\item The diagonals are perpendicular on both rhombuses and kites. Students might struggle to come up with the kite if they draw only rhombuses (or only diagonals that bisect one another), so you might want to give hints as you walk around. Some students may also bring up squares here, so this is a good opportunity to remind ourselves that squares are a special case of rhombuses.
	\item When the diagonals are perpendicular bisectors, the shape must be a rhombus, and we can use SAS to explain why this is true. Some students will want to use SSS here because they are trying to show that this is a rhombus, but we don't know that the sides are all equal until we prove this is so. You might ask why we can't use SSS as an extra thought question or if you have a group who realizes we can't proceed this way they could present.
	\item To show that a rhombus is a parallelogram, we will need both SSS (with the result of the previous problem) and the converse of the parallel postulate. This is a more difficult argument and should be treated with care. However, this result demonstrates how we can use previous results to build up more complicated results.
	\item The fourth problem should be a matter of recognizing that all squares are also rhombuses, so if a property is true of rhombuses then it must also be true of squares. The language for this ``special case'' idea can be challenging for students. Encourage similar language like ``all -- are --'' or ``some -- are --''. Another way to think about it is ``every -- has to be a --''. Whatever language the students can make sense of (and is correct) is good! You might discuss the statement: ``All (blank) are (blank)'' as well as ``Some (blank) are (blank)'' and compare and contrast the results.
	\item If you have a lot of extra time, this can be a great place to bring back the idea of Venn Diagrams to organize how the categories of shapes are related.

\end{itemize}




{\bf Good language:} Continue to have students identify when they are using a definition and when they are using a property. Also continue to emphasize that the point here is not memorizing the criteria but showing how this idea commonly discussed in high school helps us to continue to explore conjectures that we have made about relationships between shapes or the way we have categorized shapes.

{\bf Suggested timing:} Give students 10-15 minutes to discuss all of the problems in their groups, and then use the remaining time for presentations and discussion.


\end{instructorNotes}
